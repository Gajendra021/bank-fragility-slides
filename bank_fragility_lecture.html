Part 2: Theory I â€” Diamond-Dybvig

The Diamond-Dybvig Setup

A Three-Period World

A bank invests $1 at \(t=0\) in a project:

  • Pays \(R > 1\) at \(t=2\) if held to maturity
  • Pays only \(r_1 < 1\) if liquidated early at \(t=1\) (fire-sale loss)

Two types of depositors:

  • Impatient (\(\lambda\) fraction): Must consume at \(t=1\) â€” always withdraw early
  • Patient (\(1-\lambda\) fraction): Can wait until \(t=2\) â€” but may withdraw early if worried

The Multiple Equilibria Problem

Good Equilibrium

Patient depositors wait. Bank earns full return \(R\). Everyone is better off.

Bad Equilibrium (Run)

Patient depositors fear others will withdraw. All withdraw at \(t=1\). Bank liquidates at fire-sale prices. Bank may fail.

Self-fulfilling panic.

. . .

Both equilibria exist. Theory alone cannot tell you which will occur. We need more structure.

Strategic Complementarities: The Core Mechanism

What Are Strategic Complementarities?

My payoff from withdrawing depends on how many others withdraw.

  • If others run â†’ fire-sale losses â†’ less for remaining depositors â†’ I should also run
  • Running is optimal if I believe others will run
  • Not running is optimal if I believe others wonâ€™t run
  • Circular reasoning â†’ multiple equilibria (DD) or unique threshold (GP)

Part 3: Theory II â€” Global Games

Goldstein & Pauzner (2005): The Fix

The Problem with Diamond-Dybvig

Multiple equilibria â†’ the model cannot predict when runs occur. Not testable.

The Global Games Solution (Morris & Shin 2000)

Assume depositors receive slightly noisy private signals about bank performance \(\theta\).

With small noise, the multiple equilibria collapse into a unique threshold equilibrium:

\[\text{Patient depositors run if signal} < P^*\] \[\text{Patient depositors wait if signal} \geq P^*\]

\(P^*\) is the panic threshold â€” pinned down by fundamentals and the structure of the game.

Why Noise Selects a Unique Equilibrium

  • Without noise: I face strategic uncertainty â€” what will others do?
  • With slightly noisy signals: I can reason about the distribution of othersâ€™ beliefs
  • This â€œinfectionâ€ of beliefs pins down unique behavior

. . .

Key Parameter

\(r_1\) = how much the bank promises to pay early withdrawers per dollar invested

Higher \(r_1\) â†’ Higher \(P^*\) â†’ Bank is easier to run on

The Central Theoretical Prediction

The Key Comparative Static

\[\frac{\partial P^*}{\partial r_1} > 0\]

As liquidity mismatch increases (\(r_1 \uparrow\)), the run threshold rises (\(P^* \uparrow\)).

Intuition: If the bank has promised a lot of liquid claims, depositors know a run would be very damaging â€” fear of others running is more rational. Strategic complementarities are stronger.

Reading the diagram:

  • Below \(\underline{P}\): Bank insolvent â†’ fundamental run
  • Between \(\underline{P}\) and \(P^*\): Bank solvent but panic occurs
  • Above \(P^*\): No run â€” only impatient depositors withdraw

The Formal Sensitivity Derivation

Setting Up the Derivation

Consider banks with mismatch \(r_1\), run threshold \(P^*(r_1)\), and performance pdf \(f(P)\).

A small negative shock \(-\Delta P\) triggers runs at banks where \(P\) was just above \(P^*(r_1)\).

Aggregate change in flows: \[\Delta \text{FLOW}(r_1) = -[F(P^*+\Delta P) - F(P^*)](1-\lambda)\]

Dividing by \(-\Delta P\) and taking limits: \[AvgFPS(r_1) = f(P^*(r_1)) \cdot (1-\lambda)\]

Taking the Derivative w.r.t. Mismatch

\[\frac{\partial \, AvgFPS(r_1)}{\partial r_1} = (1-\lambda) \cdot f'(P^*) \cdot \frac{\partial P^*}{\partial r_1}\]

This is positive if and only if:

  1. \(\frac{\partial P^*}{\partial r_1} > 0\) â† From GP theory âœ“
  2. \(f'(P^*) > 0\) â† Thresholds are in the increasing part of the ROA distribution

Condition 2 is Empirically Verified

Figure 2 of the paper shows ROA is roughly bell-shaped. Uninsured deposits become sensitive to ROA only below the median â€” where density is still rising. So \(P^* <\) median ROA and \(f'(P^*) > 0\) âœ“

Part 4: Theory â†’ Empirics

Translating Theory into Testable Predictions

The Problem

We cannot directly observe \(P^*\) (the run threshold).

We cannot observe whether a run is â€œpanicâ€ vs. â€œfundamentals.â€

The Solution

Use cross-sectional variation in liquidity mismatch (\(r_1\)) to test the comparative static \(\frac{\partial P^*}{\partial r_1} > 0\).

The Logic

  • Banks with more mismatch â†’ higher \(P^*\)
  • Same bad performance â†’ more likely to breach the higher \(P^*\)
  • â†’ Stronger deposit outflows in expectation

Prediction 1: Sensitivity

The flow-performance sensitivity of uninsured deposits should be higher for banks with more liquidity mismatch.

\[\frac{\partial^2 \Delta Dep^U}{\partial \text{ROA} \cdot \partial r_1} > 0\]

Prediction 2: Levels

Conditional on below-median performance, banks with more liquidity mismatch should have lower (more negative) uninsured deposit flows.

\[\Delta Dep^U \big|_{\text{low ROA, high } r_1} \;<\; \Delta Dep^U \big|_{\text{low ROA, low } r_1}\]

Why Focus on Uninsured Deposits?

Insured Depositors Donâ€™t Panic

Insured depositors (up to FDIC limit) have no incentive to run â€” the government guarantees their money.

Strategic complementarities only matter for uninsured depositors.

Uninsured depositors face a prisonerâ€™s dilemma: if others run before them, they may lose money. This is the panic incentive.

Measuring Liquidity Mismatch â€” Liability Side

An uninsured depositorâ€™s incentive to run depends not just on asset quality, but on how many fellow depositors are also uninsured.

Possible Criticisms

Measurement Concerns

  • %Uninsured is endogenous â€” banks perceived as risky may attract fewer uninsured depositors (reverse causality). Addressed partially by three-year averaging.
  • Asset Illiquidity may proxy unobserved quality â€” riskier banks may take on illiquid loans and be fragile for non-panic reasons.
  • Quarterly data misses the fastest runs â€” bank runs can happen in days (cf. SVB 2023). Only persistent outflows are captured.
  • No depositor-level data â€” net flows mix entry, exit, and rate-seeking behavior. Cannot isolate true panic decisions.

Identification Concerns

  • ROA informativeness â€” the peer capital and matched-sample tests address this, but residual concerns remain about correlated unobservables.
  • Customer relationships as confound â€” banks with high mismatch might also have weaker depositor relationships and lower switching costs, not panic per se.
  • Mismatch is not exogenous â€” banks choose their liability structure. High %Uninsured may reflect risk appetite rather than structural fragility.
  • General equilibrium effects ignored â€” the paper studies individual banks. Systemic costs of fragility across the whole sector are not captured.

Key Takeaways

Methodological Lessons

  1. Theory â†’ Empirics: Derive testable predictions from theory, not just correlations. The GP model gave them the interaction term to test.
  2. Multiple tests: One regression proves little. Convergence across many designs is compelling.
  3. Falsification: Insured deposits as a placebo â€” if the story were wrong, both groups would look the same.
  4. Matched sample: When worried about confounds, match on the confound.
  5. Cross-sectional identification: Use structural variation (mismatch) to identify mechanisms when you canâ€™t randomize.

Substantive Lessons

  1. Panic is real and quantifiable â€” not just a theoretical curiosity.
  2. Liquidity mismatch is the mechanism â€” asset-side illiquidity and liability-side uninsured deposits contribute independently.
  3. Deposit insurance helps but doesnâ€™t solve â€” it changes the form of the cost.
  4. Systematic shocks are disproportionately dangerous â€” 7â€“8Ã— more damaging in a systemic crisis.
  5. Theory and empirics are complements â€” this paper would be impossible without GP (2005), and GP was an untested conjecture without this paper.
